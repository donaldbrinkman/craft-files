# Figures of speech: the surprise family

*Distilled from Mark Forsyth, The Elements of Eloquence (Icon Books, 2013). Five figures whose effect depends on the reader expecting one thing and getting another. Reach for these when a sentence needs to land harder than its content alone would manage. They pair naturally with a flow pass (see the Williams file) and a structural pass (see the Truby file).*

*The examples below are for calibration, not for lifting. The figures work when they emerge from your argument, not when they are imposed on it.*

---

## How to use this file

Each figure has the same four parts: definition, when to reach for it, how it tends to fail, and an example.

The surprise family shares a structure: the figure sets up an expectation in the first half and breaks it in the second. The break is the whole job. If the second half is what the reader already expected, the figure has not fired and the sentence is just a sentence.

Reach for these at load-bearing moments: openings, closings, the central turn, sentences that have to do the work of several. Avoid them in connective tissue, where they call attention to themselves and slow the reader down.

---

## Paraprosdokian

**Definition.** A sentence whose second half forces you to reinterpret the first. The setup leans the reader one way; the payoff reveals the lean was wrong, and the reader has to go back and re-read.

**When to reach for it.** Closing lines. Section-ending beats. Anywhere a sentence should land with a small jolt of recognition. It works well when an argument has been building one direction and the close wants to acknowledge a complication without abandoning the point.

**How it tends to fail.** The setup was not actually leaning anywhere, so the second half reverses nothing. Or the reversal is too clever and the reader re-reads out of duty rather than pleasure. Test it in isolation: does the first half create an expectation strong enough for the second half to break cleanly?

**Example.** "She was a brilliant manager, right up until anyone disagreed with her."

The setup (brilliant manager) sounds like praise. The break (right up until anyone disagreed) recasts it. The reader re-reads the first half in a new light.

---

## Zeugma

**Definition.** One verb governs two or more objects, and its meaning shifts between them. The single verb does double or triple duty across registers that would not normally share it.

**When to reach for it.** When you want one sentence to compress two ideas into one verb's reach. It is especially useful when one object is literal and another is figurative: the figure quietly claims that both are governed by the same logic, without having to argue it.

**How it tends to fail.** The registers are too far apart and the sentence reads as a pun. Or they are too close and the shift goes unnoticed, so the figure does no work. The shift should be visible without being garish.

**Example.** "He lost his keys, his temper, and any hope of catching the early train."

"Lost" governs three objects: one literal (keys), one idiomatic (temper), one figurative (hope). The figurative object carries the feeling; the literal one makes it land.

---

## Antanaclasis

**Definition.** A word repeats in close succession with a different meaning each time. Same word, different load. The repetition is the figure; the meaning shift is the payoff.

**When to reach for it.** When you are making a point about how the same surface can hide different logics depending on context. The figure performs the argument in miniature: the surface (the repeated word) stays the same; the meaning underneath changes. Useful for openings that want to plant an idea without stating it outright.

**How it tends to fail.** The two meanings are too similar and the repetition reads as redundancy. Or too different, and it reads as wordplay rather than a point. The figure works when the gap between the two meanings is exactly the size of the idea it is performing.

**Example.** "The interview is there to test whether you can test your own assumptions."

"Test" appears twice: first as assess, then as challenge. The shift is the point of the sentence.

---

## Chiasmus

**Definition.** A sentence or sentence-pair whose second half inverts the order of the first. An ABBA pattern. The mirrored structure gives the figure a sense of balance or completion.

**When to reach for it.** Thesis sentences. Closing lines. Anywhere you want a claim to feel earned by its shape. Chiasmus makes an argument feel inevitable because the structure mirrors itself, and the reader registers the mirror before they consciously notice it.

**How it tends to fail.** The two halves do not really invert; they just rephrase. Or the inversion is mechanical and reads as an exercise. The figure earns its place when the second half means the inverse of the first, not the same thing said backward.

**Example.** "Good writing makes hard ideas easy. Bad writing makes easy ideas hard."

The order inverts (good, hard, easy / bad, easy, hard) and the meaning inverts with it. The balance lands because the contrast is real.

---

## Antithesis

**Definition.** Two ideas set in deliberate opposition, usually in parallel grammatical structure. The contrast is the figure; the parallelism makes it feel structural rather than decorative.

**When to reach for it.** When an argument has a clean two-sided shape and you want both sides to land at once. It is the figure that does the most to set up later argument: it puts both halves on the table so you can spend the next paragraph complicating one of them.

**How it tends to fail.** False antithesis, where the two halves are merely adjacent rather than opposed. Or forced antithesis, where the grammar is parallel but the ideas do not pull against each other hard enough to justify the figure. It works when the opposition is load-bearing, not when it is ornamental.

**Example.** "The resume opens the door. The interview decides whether you walk through it."

Two halves, parallel shape, real opposition (opening versus deciding). The figure does in one breath what plain prose would take a paragraph to do.

---

## How to layer these in

In a revision pass, work the figures in this order:

1. **Antithesis first.** Most arguments have a load-bearing two-sided structure somewhere. Find the sentence where the central tension lives and see whether it wants to be antithesis. It carries the most weight per sentence of any figure in this family.

2. **Chiasmus at structural moments.** Section openings and closings, the final sentence. It rewards readers who notice it without punishing those who do not. Use it sparingly: once per piece is usually right, twice is the ceiling.

3. **Paraprosdokian at closings.** End-of-paragraph and end-of-section beats. The reversal is often the closing's whole job.

4. **Antanaclasis where the argument doubles back.** Most pieces say a word in one sense and re-use it in another as the argument develops. Notice those moments and tighten them.

5. **Zeugma sparingly.** The most fragile of the five; small calibration errors push it from argument into wordplay. Reserve it for moments where the compression is doing real work.

---

## Things to flinch at

- **The figure draws attention to itself.** If the reader notices the figure before the point, it is overdressed. The sentence should land as an argument first; only on a second look should the reader register the move.

- **The figure is correct in form but empty in content.** A chiasmus that mirrors perfectly but says nothing new. An antithesis between two things that do not really oppose. The figure is scaffolding; the argument is what gets built on it. Empty scaffolding is just scaffolding.

- **The figure cascades.** Two paraprosdokian closings in a row teach the reader to expect them, which kills both. Stacked antithesis becomes a list dressed as an argument. These figures work by interrupting a pattern, not by becoming one.

- **The figure is in the wrong register.** A paraprosdokian dropped into plain expository prose lands as a swerve the reader did not sign up for. A chiasmus in a casual aside reads as showing off. The figure should feel like a slight intensification of the prose around it, not a register change.

---

## When to leave them alone

Some passages do not want figures:

- **Connective tissue.** The sentences that move the reader from one paragraph to the next should be invisible. Figures here pull focus.

- **Specific factual claims.** "We shipped on the 14th" does not want a figure. Reach for figures at the moments that interpret facts, not the moments that report them.

- **Quotations.** Someone else's words are someone else's words. Do not lay your figures over a quote. Quote it clean.

The figures are tools for the moments where prose has to do extra work. Most of a piece is moments where prose only has to do its normal work. Do not lay scaffolding where no building is going up.

---

## What this file does not cover

Forsyth's book runs to thirty-nine figures across several families: surprise (covered here), sound (alliteration, assonance), repetition (anaphora, epistrophe, polysyndeton), naming and substitution (metonymy, synecdoche, hendiadys), inversion (hyperbaton), and more. The surprise family is the highest-leverage subset for argumentative writing with structural turns. For figures outside this family, Silva Rhetoricae (rhetoric.byu.edu) is a free, comprehensive reference, organized by both name and function.

## A note to the AI assistant using this file

When you apply these techniques to a piece of writing, end your response with a short note telling the user which moves you used and why. Name the two or three changes that did the most work, plainly, in a sentence or two. The point is to teach as you edit, so the user starts to see the moves for themselves.

Keep it brief and high level. Do not narrate every sentence or list every technique; a long audit buries the writing it is meant to explain. If the user has given you more than one of these craft files, write one combined note for the whole edit, not one note per file.
