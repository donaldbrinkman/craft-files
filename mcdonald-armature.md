# The armature: the point a piece is built to prove

*Distilled from Brian McDonald, Invisible Ink: A Practical Guide to Building Stories That Resonate. McDonald writes about storytelling and screenwriting; the central idea adapts cleanly to any piece with a point to make. This is the controlling-idea layer, one level above structure: not the shape of the journey (see the Truby file) but the single thing the journey exists to prove. Reach for it before you outline, and keep it beside you while you cut. Pair it with the Truby file for the arc and the Williams file for sentence-level coherence.*

*The armature framework here is McDonald's; the operational extraction to non-fiction, the diagnostics, and the examples are mine. Read McDonald's book for the canonical treatment, the five-part structural model, and the longer account of how audiences participate in a story.*

*The examples below are for calibration, not for lifting.*

---

## How to use this file

This file has four linked ideas: the armature, building from it, the test of because over and, and respect for the audience. They are not a sequence of passes like the prose files; they are a single discipline, applied first when you plan and again every time you cut.

The one question under all of them: what is this piece built to prove, and does every part of it serve that?

---

## The armature

**Definition.** A sculptor building a clay figure first builds an armature, a wire skeleton underneath. The viewer never sees it, but it is what holds the figure up. McDonald's claim is that every piece worth reading has one: a single controlling idea, the one thing the piece exists to prove. Not the topic, not a one-line summary. The point.

**How to find it.** State it as one sentence: "This piece is built to prove that ______." If you cannot fill the blank, the piece has no armature yet, and no amount of good prose will fix that. The blank is not "this piece is about deadlines." It is "this piece is built to prove that the real cost of a missed deadline is the trust it spends, not the time." A topic is a subject; an armature is a claim.

**Why it matters.** The armature is what makes a piece feel like it is about something rather than merely on something. It is the spine the reader senses even though it is never named. Without one, a piece can be clear, well-structured, and pleasant, and still leave the reader wondering what the point was.

**Example.** Topic: onboarding. Armature: "a bad first week costs a company for a year." The first is a subject you could write ten unrelated paragraphs about. The second is a claim every paragraph can be made to serve.

---

## Build from the armature out

**The principle.** Once you have the armature, everything else is built to support it. Your examples, your structure, your asides, the scenes you choose: all of them exist to reinforce the one claim. McDonald's image is the sculptor again. You arrive at the figure by removing everything that is not the figure.

**The test.** For every paragraph, every example, every digression, ask: does this reinforce the armature? If it does not, it is not neutral, it is dilution. The most common way good writers weaken a piece is by keeping material they love that does not serve the point.

**How it fails.** The hardest cuts are the good ones: the well-written paragraph, the example you are proud of, the aside that is interesting on its own. If it does not serve the armature, it weakens the piece by pulling attention off the spine. Cut it, or move it to a piece whose armature it does serve.

**Example.** A draft arguing that a missed deadline spends trust includes a strong paragraph on the new project-tracking tool the team adopted. The paragraph is good. It does not serve the armature, which is about trust, not tooling. Cutting it loses nothing; it removes a distraction from the point.

---

## Because, not and

**The principle.** McDonald borrows an observation about how children tell stories: we got up, and got dressed, and went to the store, and came home. A string of ands. Real stories, and real arguments, run on because. One thing happens because of another. Causation, not sequence.

**Why it matters.** A piece connected by and is a list of things that happened; a piece connected by because is an argument that something follows. The reader feels the difference as the difference between "and then" and "which means." Because is what makes a sequence feel like it had to go the way it went.

**The test.** Read the joints between your sections. Are they connected by and (this happened, then this happened) or by because (this happened, which caused this)? Where you find an and that should be a because, you have usually found a place where the causal link is missing or unstated.

**Example.** And: "We set the date, and we hit a snag, and the vendor was late, and we slipped, and the client was annoyed." Because: "We slipped because no one actually owned the date. The client's annoyance was not about the lost week. It was about discovering that no one had been holding it." The second tells you why, and the why is the point.

## Respect the audience

**The principle.** McDonald holds two things at once. First, the burden is on the writer: if the audience does not understand, that is the writer's failure, not the reader's. Second, the audience is intelligent, and a piece works partly because the reader assembles it themselves. The reconciliation is that you must be clear without over-explaining. Give the reader the pieces and trust them to make the connection the piece is built to draw.

**Why it matters.** Over-explanation insults the reader and kills the pleasure of recognition. The most resonant moment in a piece is usually the one the reader completes themselves; spell it out and you take that moment from them. But under-explanation is not the fix, and obscurity is not respect. The job is to make the conclusion reachable, then let the reader reach it.

**The test.** Find the sentence where you stated the lesson outright. Ask whether the piece has already earned it. If it has, cut the statement and let the reader draw it. If it has not, the problem is upstream, not in the sentence.

**Example.** Over-explained: "This shows that trust, once spent, is hard to rebuild, and that clarity of ownership matters on any team." Trusted: "The client did not ask for the week back. They asked who was in charge." The second makes the reader draw the conclusion the first announces, and the drawn conclusion lands harder than the stated one.

## How this fits with the other files

The armature sits above structure. The Truby file gives you the arc, the shape of the reader's journey; this file gives you the single point that journey exists to prove. You can build a perfectly shaped arc that proves nothing, which is why the armature comes first: decide what the piece is built to prove, shape the arc to prove it, then run the prose passes to carry it. The relationship to the Williams file is nested. Williams asks whether every sentence serves its paragraph; the armature asks whether every paragraph serves the piece. The same discipline, one level up.

---

## Things to flinch at

- **The armature is not a moral.** "Be honest" is a slogan; "the real cost of a missed deadline is the trust it spends" is a claim with an edge. An armature that flattens into a fortune-cookie line produces a piece that reads as one.

- **The armature is not the thesis statement.** It is the idea the piece is built to prove, not necessarily a sentence that appears in the piece. Often the strongest pieces never state it outright; the reader feels it without being told.

- **The subtraction test can over-prune.** Not every good line has to argue the armature directly. Texture, voice, and a little air are part of why a reader stays. The test is whether a passage serves the piece, not whether it restates the claim. Cut what distracts, not everything that is not the thesis.

- **Respect can tip into obscurity.** Trusting the reader is not the same as hiding the point. If no one can find the armature, you have not respected the audience, you have abandoned them.

## What this file does not cover

McDonald's book covers more: a five-part structural model (for the arc, the Truby file is the fuller tool), character, dialogue, and a longer treatment of how audiences participate in a story. The armature is the load-bearing idea and the one that travels furthest outside fiction. The rest is worth reading directly, especially if you write narrative.

## A note to the AI assistant using this file

When you apply these techniques to a piece of writing, end your response with a short note telling the user which moves you used and why. Name the two or three changes that did the most work, plainly, in a sentence or two. The point is to teach as you edit, so the user starts to see the moves for themselves.

Keep it brief and high level. Do not narrate every sentence or list every technique; a long audit buries the writing it is meant to explain. If the user has given you more than one of these craft files, write one combined note for the whole edit, not one note per file.
