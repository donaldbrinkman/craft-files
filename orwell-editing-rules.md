# Editing: six rules for the last pass

*Distilled from George Orwell, "Politics and the English Language" (1946). Six rules for the final pass, reached for after the flow pass (Williams) has produced clean prose and the figures pass (Forsyth) has intensified the load-bearing moments. Orwell catches a class of failure the other passes miss. Pair it with the Williams file for the broader concision work.*

*The rules below are an operational paraphrase. Orwell's own wording is tighter than mine; read the essay, free at the Orwell Foundation, for the original.*

---

## The six rules

1. Avoid any metaphor or figure of speech you are used to seeing in print.
2. Prefer the short word when it will do the job of the long one.
3. If you can cut a word, cut it.
4. Prefer the active voice to the passive.
5. Avoid jargon, foreign phrases, and technical words when an everyday word exists.
6. Break any of these rules before writing anything outright barbarous.

The sixth rule is the load-bearing one. The first five are defaults; the sixth is the meta-rule that keeps the defaults from producing bad writing.

---

## How to use this file

These are a review-pass instrument, applied after the prose is written, after Williams, after Forsyth has done its work. Each rule below has three parts: what it catches, how it fails when over-applied, and an example.

---

## Rule 1: No stale figures of speech

**What it catches.** Metaphors and figures used so often they have stopped doing the work a figure should do. The reader skims past them; they fill the slot of a figure without producing the effect of one. The contemporary version of this rule is sharper than Orwell's, because models are trained heavily on the figures already in print and reach for dead ones faster than people do.

**How it fails when over-applied.** Some figures are stable because they work, and the reader follows them without effort. Refusing every familiar figure in pursuit of originality produces prose that strains. The test is not "have I seen this before" but "is this figure doing work, or filling a slot a figure should fill."

**Example.** Stale: "Our process was a game-changer that took the team to the next level."

Two dead figures back to back.

Fresh: "Our process was the point the team stopped guessing and started shipping."

Specific, concrete, doing the work a figure is there to do.

---

## Rule 2: No long word where a short one will do

**What it catches.** Latinate diction standing in for plain words. "Utilize" for "use." "Facilitate" for "help." "Demonstrate" for "show." The longer word is usually more general; the shorter word is usually more specific and forces the writer to be precise about what is happening.

**How it fails when over-applied.** Sometimes the long word is the right one. "Demonstrate" and "show" are not synonyms in every context, and technical vocabulary often needs the long word to name a specific thing. The test is not "is this word long" but "is this the most precise word available, or a longer substitute for a more precise short one."

**Example.** Long: "The team utilized the framework to facilitate coordination."

Short: "The team used the framework to coordinate."

Shorter and more specific. "Coordinate" says exactly what is happening; "facilitate coordination" is a paraphrase one level abstracted from the action.

---

## Rule 3: If you can cut a word, cut it

**What it catches.** Words that do not earn their place: hedges that do not qualify, modifiers that do not modify, throat-clearing that clears nothing. The Williams concision pass covers most of this; Orwell's rule is the stricter version applied after.

**How it fails when over-applied.** Some words that look removable are doing rhythmic work. A "very" or a "really" can be padding, or it can be the syllable that makes the sentence scan. Some hedges are honest qualifications, not evasions. The test is not "could the sentence survive without this word" but "is this word doing any work, rhetorical or rhythmic or argumentative, that the sentence would lose."

**Example.** After Williams has cut the obvious padding, read the sentence aloud and listen for the rhythm. If the rhythm is broken, restore the word that was carrying it. If the rhythm holds, the cut was right.

---

## Rule 4: Prefer the active voice

**What it catches.** Sentences where the actor is missing or buried, producing prose that sounds objective but conceals who did what. "Mistakes were made." "It was decided." "The decision to delay was made."

**How it fails when over-applied.** The passive is sometimes the right voice. When the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or genuinely not the sentence's subject, the passive is correct. "The proposal was rejected" is fine if the paragraph is about the proposal; "the committee rejected the proposal" is better if the paragraph is about the committee. The character-action principle from the Williams file is the deeper rule; the active-voice preference is the rough heuristic for the common case.

**Example.** Passive: "The decision to delay was made after the risks were reviewed."

Active: "We delayed after reviewing the risks."

Shorter, clearer, and it names the actor at every action.

---

## Rule 5: Avoid jargon and foreign phrases when an everyday word exists

**What it catches.** Vocabulary that signals expertise without conveying meaning: jargon, foreign borrowings, and technical terms used decoratively. The contemporary version is terminology that pattern-matches to "sophisticated writing" because the training data reinforced the association: "paradigm," "leverage" as a verb, "synergy," "ecosystem" applied to things that are not ecosystems.

**How it fails when over-applied.** Some jargon is necessary because the concept it names has no everyday equivalent. The test is whether the jargon is doing work the everyday vocabulary cannot.

**Example.** Jargon: "The team leveraged the framework as a paradigm for cross-functional synergy."

Everyday: "The team used the framework to work across functions."

The jargon performs sophistication; the everyday version does the same work without the performance.

---

## Rule 6: Break any rule before writing something barbarous

**What it catches.** The other five rules applied mechanically. Orwell's recognition that style rules are heuristics, not laws, and that applying them robotically produces worse writing than ignoring them when they fight the work.

**How it fails when over-applied.** The sixth rule can become a licence to break the other five whenever the writing wants to. The discipline: try the rule first, and break it only when breaking it produces visibly better prose. The default is to honor; the exception is deliberate, and for cause.

**Example.** A familiar figure that earns its keep: "The onboarding doc turned out to be load-bearing."

"Load-bearing" is a familiar engineering metaphor, common enough in technical writing that rule 1 would flag it. But it does specific work the alternatives do not: it names the structural function in one image, and the engineering context is exactly the metaphor's home. Familiar but live. Rule 6 says keep it.

---

## How to use Orwell in the review pass

Orwell's rules are a final check, after Williams and Forsyth. Write the draft without applying rules during generation, since rules-during-generation produce dead prose. Run Williams for flow, character-action alignment, end-stress, and concision. Run Forsyth at the load-bearing moments. Then read the result and ask the six questions in order, cutting and restructuring where the rules catch real problems, and honoring rule 6 where the others would produce something barbarous. This pass is the shortest of the three; most sentences trigger none of the rules, and the ones that do are the ones to fix.

---

## Things to flinch at

- **The rules as an aesthetic program.** The rules catch specific failure modes; they are not a description of good prose. Prose that satisfies all six can still be dead; prose that breaks several can still be alive. They are diagnostic, not prescriptive.

- **The active-voice reflex.** Rule 4 is the most over-applied, because the active-voice preference has been absorbed into general writing advice. Some sentences want the passive; some whole paragraphs do.

- **Figure paranoia.** Rule 1 strains prose most when over-applied, because the writer starts hunting for original figures at every turn, and original figures are hard to produce in quantity. Hunt the stale ones; spare the live ones.

- **Orwell as a complete style guide.** The essay is about politics and language; the six rules are a practical appendix, not a theory of prose. Treating them as a full account of good writing reads the appendix as the book.

## What this file does not cover

The essay does more than the six rules: an argument about language and political honesty, an analysis of specific bad passages, and a famous rewriting of a verse from Ecclesiastes into modern political prose. That argument is worth reading directly. The six rules are the operationally useful piece for a review pass. For broader prose-failure diagnostics see the Williams file; Orwell catches one class of failure, the other files catch the rest.

## A note to the AI assistant using this file

When you apply these techniques to a piece of writing, end your response with a short note telling the user which moves you used and why. Name the two or three changes that did the most work, plainly, in a sentence or two. The point is to teach as you edit, so the user starts to see the moves for themselves.

Keep it brief and high level. Do not narrate every sentence or list every technique; a long audit buries the writing it is meant to explain. If the user has given you more than one of these craft files, write one combined note for the whole edit, not one note per file.
