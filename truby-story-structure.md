# Story structure: seven steps

*Distilled from John Truby, The Anatomy of Story (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2007). The seven key structure steps from Truby's larger framework, adapted from fiction to argumentative non-fiction. Reach for this during the structural pass, before any sentence-level work. The seven steps map an argument's arc the way Truby maps a story's; the mapping is approximate but operational. Pair it with the Williams file for prose and the Forsyth file for the sentences that have to land.*

*The seven-step framework here is Truby's; the operational adaptation to non-fiction, the diagnostics, and the examples are mine. Read Truby's book for the canonical framework, the full character and symbol webs, the designing principle, and a much deeper treatment than any short distillation can offer.*

---

## How to use this file

Truby's framework is for fiction. The seven steps below are adapted for argumentative essays with structural turns and a thesis that develops across the piece. The adaptation is not strict; Truby is the source of the vocabulary, and the application is different.

The seven steps, in Truby's order, and their non-fiction mapping:

1. Weakness and need: the gap in the reader's current understanding.
2. Desire: the question the reader brings.
3. Opponent: the view the piece argues against.
4. Plan: the piece's argumentative strategy.
5. Battle: the central, hardest argumentative moment.
6. Self-revelation: the reader's moment of recognition.
7. New equilibrium: the understanding the reader carries away.

Each entry has the same shape: the step in Truby's terms, the non-fiction adaptation, the diagnostic, and an illustration. The illustration runs through one example essay, arguing that a small team ships faster by hiring less, so you can watch the arc assemble.

---

## Step 1: Weakness and need

**Truby.** The hero begins with a flaw that holds them back and a need, often unconscious, that the story will surface. The flaw is shown through behavior; the need is what the story resolves.

**Non-fiction adaptation.** The reader begins with an incomplete or incorrect understanding. The weakness is the gap in their current frame; the need is the deeper understanding the piece provides.

**Diagnostic.** Can you name what the reader does not currently understand, and why their current frame is failing them? If the answer is only "they do not know about this topic," the piece is informational, not argumentative. An argument requires a failure in the reader's frame, not just an absence of information.

**Illustration.** The reader assumes a team that is behind should add people. That frame is failing them: it explains the symptom (too much work) but not the cause (coordination cost). The need is to see headcount as a cost as well as a capacity.

---

## Step 2: Desire

**Truby.** The hero's conscious goal, the thing they are trying to achieve. The desire drives the plot.

**Non-fiction adaptation.** The reader's conscious question, the thing they came to learn. The desire is what makes them start reading and keeps them going until the turn.

**Diagnostic.** Can you name the question the reader arrives with? Not the one you want them to leave with, the one they bring. If you cannot name it, the piece will fight their attention.

**Illustration.** The reader's question is practical: how do I get my team unstuck? The piece answers that on the way to the deeper point, which is that the instinct to answer it with hiring is the problem.

---

## Step 3: Opponent

**Truby.** The character or force that wants to stop the hero. Not always a villain; sometimes a system, a circumstance, or an internal flaw. The opponent makes the desire hard.

**Non-fiction adaptation.** The conventional wisdom or competing argument the piece pushes against. Without an opponent the piece preaches to the choir; with one, it earns its claims through contrast.

**Diagnostic.** Can you name the position the piece argues against, in its strongest form? A strawmanned opponent makes the argument unearned.

**Illustration.** The opponent is the common-sense view at its strongest: more capacity means more output, and a team behind on work obviously needs more hands. The piece has to beat that version, not a weak one.

---

## Step 4: Plan

**Truby.** The hero's strategy for achieving the desire against the opponent. The plan structures the middle of the story.

**Non-fiction adaptation.** The piece's argumentative strategy: the order of moves, the evidence, the structural choices that build the case between the opening and the turn.

**Diagnostic.** Can you sketch the arc in three to five sentences? Does each sentence describe a move that advances the argument, or do the moves just elaborate the thesis?

**Illustration.** Open on the felt problem (the team is drowning). Introduce the hidden cost (every new person adds communication paths). Show a case where adding people made things slower. Land the reframe (capacity was never the constraint). Each move advances; none merely restates.

---

## Step 5: Battle

**Truby.** The confrontation between hero and opponent, the central conflict brought to a head.

**Non-fiction adaptation.** The central argumentative moment, where the opponent's position is engaged most directly and the argument does its hardest work. This is where the reader is most resistant; if the piece does not earn it, the rest collapses.

**Diagnostic.** Where is the argument's hardest sentence, the one a resistant reader is most likely to doubt or skim? That is the battle. Are you spending enough words and evidence there, or rushing through?

**Illustration.** The hardest claim is that adding good people can make a capable team slower. A reader resists this; it sounds backward. The battle has to land it with enough specifics, the math of communication paths and a concrete case, that the reader cannot wave it off as a paradox for effect.

---

## Step 6: Self-revelation

**Truby.** The hero's moment of recognition, seeing something they could not see before. It is the story's emotional payoff, what the audience came for even if they did not know it.

**Non-fiction adaptation.** The reader's moment of recognition, where the frame they came in with cracks and the new frame locks in. The self-revelation is what the piece is for.

**Diagnostic.** Can you name the sentence where the reader's understanding shifts? If you cannot, the piece may have no turn. If you can, does it have the weight, placement and prose and evidence behind it, to actually produce the shift?

**Illustration.** The turn is the sentence that recasts the whole problem: the team was never short on capacity, it was short on focus, and hiring buys capacity at the cost of focus. It comes after the battle has earned it, and it lands in one sentence, not three.

---

## Step 7: New equilibrium

**Truby.** The resolution, the hero's world after the recognition. Not necessarily happy, but stable; the change is integrated, for better or worse.

**Non-fiction adaptation.** The reader's understanding after the turn. The close makes the new frame feel stable: earned, applicable beyond the piece, something to carry. This is what makes the piece worth re-reading.

**Diagnostic.** What does the reader believe after reading that they did not believe before? Can you state it in one sentence? If it is fuzzy, the close is not doing its job.

**Illustration.** The reader leaves believing that headcount is a tool with a cost, not a default answer, and that focus is the thing to protect. They came in equating "behind" with "understaffed"; they leave with a sharper model. The close lands that without flattening it into a slogan.

---

## How to use the seven steps in the layering pass

The seven steps are most useful before the prose-level passes. Outline against them first; sketch the structure as seven moves before drafting. If a step is missing or weak, the structure has a hole. Then draft against the outline, making each step present rather than beautiful. Then run Williams for sentence-level clarity, Forsyth to intensify the load-bearing moments, and Orwell as the final edit. Applying Truby late produces structural changes that ripple through polished prose; applying it first keeps you from polishing prose into a shape that does not work.

---

## Things to flinch at

- **The fiction-template error.** The framework is for fiction. Forced into a strict adaptation, it produces essays that read as overwrought. The seven steps are a vocabulary for thinking about structure, not a template to fill in.

- **The opponent as villain.** In fiction the opponent is often dramatized. In non-fiction the opponent is a position. Trying to dramatize it produces strawmen. The opponent is a frame, not a person.

- **The self-revelation as twist.** Truby's self-revelation is a recognition, not a plot twist. Engineering a surprise ending produces pieces that feel manipulated. The turn should feel inevitable in hindsight: "of course," not "wait, what?"

- **The new equilibrium as moral.** Closing on a tidy lesson is the writing-school trap. The new equilibrium is a stable understanding, not a slogan. Pieces that close on slogans read as engagement-bait; pieces that close on integrated understanding read as earned.

## What this file does not cover

Truby's full framework includes substantial material on character webs, symbol webs, scene weaves, and the designing principle that organizes a story at the highest level. The seven key steps are the load-bearing subset for argumentative writing. The rest is worth reading directly for longer or more intricate work. For sentence-level structure see the Williams file; for figure-level intensification see the Forsyth file; for the final edit see the Orwell file. Truby handles structure; the others handle what it carries.

## A note to the AI assistant using this file

When you apply these techniques to a piece of writing, end your response with a short note telling the user which moves you used and why. Name the two or three changes that did the most work, plainly, in a sentence or two. The point is to teach as you edit, so the user starts to see the moves for themselves.

Keep it brief and high level. Do not narrate every sentence or list every technique; a long audit buries the writing it is meant to explain. If the user has given you more than one of these craft files, write one combined note for the whole edit, not one note per file.
