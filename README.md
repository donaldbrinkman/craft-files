# Brinkman's craft files

Five short files that distill writing-craft frameworks into operational
guidance for an AI assistant. Each file turns "here is the idea" into
"here is when to reach for it, how it fails, and what to watch out for,"
written as instructions an AI can act on and a person can learn from.

I use these to refine my own first drafts. I start with an undisciplined
stream-of-consciousness dump and ask the agent to apply the rules. The
agent revises and tells me which moves it used and why, every time. The
craft was never the scarce part. Turning it into something a machine can
act on, and a person can learn from, was.

## The files

| File | What it covers | Source |
|---|---|---|
| `forsyth-figures-of-speech.md` | Five figures of speech for moments that need to land harder than their content alone | Forsyth, *The Elements of Eloquence* |
| `williams-flow-and-cohesion.md` | Five passes for clean paragraph-level prose: character-action, old-to-new, cohesion, end-stress, concision | Williams, *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace* |
| `truby-story-structure.md` | Seven structural steps adapted from fiction to argumentative non-fiction | Truby, *The Anatomy of Story* |
| `orwell-editing-rules.md` | Six rules for the final editing pass | Orwell, "Politics and the English Language" |
| `storytelling-and-attention.md` | The four-beat attention loop and why some writing holds a reader | Loewenstein, Zak, Heath, Green and Brock, Freytag |

## How to use them

Drop one or more files into the system prompt or instructions of your
AI assistant. Then give the assistant a draft and ask it to apply the
rules. The files are written as agent instructions; the assistant
should produce a revision plus a short note naming the two or three
moves that did the most work.

The files compose well. The recommended layering for a substantive
revision pass:

1. **Truby first**, before drafting, to check the structure has a
   real turn.
2. **Williams next**, on the draft, to clean the prose at sentence
   and paragraph level.
3. **Forsyth third**, at the load-bearing moments, to intensify
   sentences that have to land harder.
4. **Orwell last**, as the final edit, to catch the failure modes
   the other passes miss.

The Storytelling and Attention file is the why-it-works layer beneath
the others; useful as background or when a piece is correct on paper
but not holding the reader.

## Credits and further reading

Each file credits its source at the top. The sources are the canonical
treatments and are worth reading directly if you want depth beyond the
operational distillation. The files are adapters, not substitutes.

- Mark Forsyth, *The Elements of Eloquence* (Icon Books, 2013). For
  the figures outside the surprise family the file covers, see also
  Silva Rhetoricae (rhetoric.byu.edu).
- Joseph Williams, *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace*. Multiple
  editions; any recent one works.
- John Truby, *The Anatomy of Story* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux,
  2007). The book treats character webs, symbol webs, and the
  designing principle beyond the seven steps; worth reading whole
  for longer or more intricate work.
- George Orwell, "Politics and the English Language" (1946). Free
  at the Orwell Foundation (orwellfoundation.com).
- The synthesis file cites George Loewenstein on the information
  gap, Paul Zak on narrative and attention, Chip and Dan Heath's
  *Made to Stick*, Melanie Green and Timothy Brock on narrative
  transportation, and Gustav Freytag on dramatic shape.
