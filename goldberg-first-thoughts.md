# First thoughts: the generation pass

*Distilled from Natalie Goldberg, Writing Down the Bones: Freeing the Writer Within, with a starting tactic from Roberta Allen's Fast Fiction. The other craft files refine, structure, and cut writing that already exists. This one is about the stage before all of that: getting the raw material onto the page. It is the front of the pipeline, and it runs on the opposite instinct from everything downstream. Reach for it first, when the page is blank.*

*The examples below are for calibration, not for lifting.*

---

## How to use this file

This file reads two ways, and both are intended.

For a person, it is a practice: how to produce raw first-draft material without the inner editor strangling it on the way out.

For an AI assistant, it is an instruction for the generation pass, and it points the opposite direction from your defaults. A model's failure mode is not a harsh inner critic; it is premature polish: smoothing, hedging, balancing, and settling into the safe middle before there is anything worth smoothing. This file says, in the first pass, do the reverse. Produce raw, specific, energetic, divergent material. Overproduce. Resist the pull toward the tidy and the average. The refinement files handle everything that comes after.

The one rule under all of it: generating and editing are different jobs, and doing them at the same time kills both.

---

## First thoughts

**The idea.** Goldberg's central term is "first thoughts": the initial, unedited perception, before the mind has had time to revise it into something more acceptable. First thoughts carry an energy and a truth that second and third thoughts sand off, because they have not yet been made polite.

**Why it matters.** The editing mind is useful later and fatal now. Applied during generation, it kills the live, strange, specific material before it reaches the page and replaces it with the expected. What survives is correct and dead.

**The discipline.** In the first pass, write what you actually see and feel, not what you think you are supposed to. Let the odd phrasing stand. The thing you are tempted to soften is often the thing worth keeping raw, at least until a later pass decides.

**Example.** First thought: "The deadline did not slip. We let go of it, one quiet rationalization at a time." Second thought: "There were a number of contributing factors to the timeline not being met." The first has a perception in it. The second has been made safe, and made dead.

---

## Keep moving; do not edit as you write

**The principle.** Goldberg's most quoted rule is to keep the hand moving and not cross out. The point is not speed for its own sake; it is the separation of two jobs. The moment you stop to fix sentence one, you have switched from generating to editing, and you will not reach the idea waiting in sentence ten.

**Why it matters.** Editing mid-generation feels productive and is not. It produces a polished opening attached to nothing, because the energy went into the polish before the material existed. Get the whole rough mass out first; shape it second.

**The discipline.** In the generation pass, do not reread, do not correct, do not optimize. Forward only. Save every judgment for a separate pass with a different goal.

**Example.** The writer who perfects the first sentence five times often never reaches the third sentence, where the actual point was hiding. The writer who pushes through ten messy sentences has something to cut down to.

---

## Go for the energy

**The principle.** Goldberg's instruction is to go toward the charged material, the thing that is a little scary or a little too alive to say plainly. That is usually where the real writing is, because it is the part you have not already automated.

**Why it matters.** The safe version of a sentence is the one everyone writes; it carries no charge because it took no risk. The generation pass is the place to take the risk, before the editing passes decide what to keep. You cannot refine energy into a draft that never had any.

**The discipline.** When a sentence makes you want to back off and generalize, that is often the signal to go closer, not further. Generalize later if you must. First, get the specific, living version down.

**Example.** Backed off: "The meeting was difficult and emotions were high." Gone toward it: "Halfway through, someone said the quiet part, and the room stopped pretending." The second risks something. The first is a weather report.

---

## Overproduce

**The principle.** Generate more than you need. Five openings, not one. The whole tangent, not the cautious half of it. Generation is cheap and cutting is easy; the expensive mistake is producing too little to choose from.

**Why it matters.** A first pass that produces exactly one safe version gives the later passes nothing to work with. They can only cut, sharpen, and choose, and they do their best work when there is a surplus to cut from. Abundance at generation is what makes ruthlessness at editing possible.

**The discipline.** In the generation pass, aim for volume and range, not quality. Quality is a later pass's job. Most of what you produce here will be cut, and that is the design, not a failure.

**Example.** Asked for an opening line, generate ten, including the bad ones and the strange ones, then choose. The line you would not have reached if you stopped at one is often the one worth keeping.

---

## Start with a constraint

**The principle.** From Roberta Allen's Fast Fiction: a blank page freezes, a constraint frees. A timer, a single prompt, a forced first word, a fixed length, any of these focuses the energy on the telling instead of on the terror of infinite choice.

**Why it matters.** Most generation stalls are not a shortage of ideas; they are a paralysis of options. Narrowing the field deliberately, "write for five minutes about the moment it went wrong," removes the paralysis and lets the material come.

**The discipline.** When generation stalls, do not wait for inspiration. Impose a constraint and start. A timed, prompted burst almost always produces more usable raw material than staring at the blank does.

**Example.** Stuck on an essay about a missed deadline, set a five-minute timer and one prompt: "the moment you knew." Write only that, fast. The opening you were failing to plan is often somewhere in the burst.

## How this fits with the other files

This is stage zero. Everything else in the set is what happens to the raw material after it exists: structure it (the Truby file), find its single point (the McDonald file), make it flow (the Williams file), intensify the load-bearing moments (the Forsyth files), keep the reader inside it (the Gardner file), and cut it hard (the Orwell file). The reason to keep this file separate from those is the cardinal rule: generation and editing are opposite motions, and the work goes better when you do not ask one pass to do both. Produce wide and raw here. Get disciplined everywhere after.

---

## Things to flinch at

- **Editing in disguise.** The urge to "just fix this one line" during generation is the editing mind sneaking back in. It will cost you the material downstream. Notice it and push forward.

- **Mistaking raw for done.** First thoughts are the start, not the finish. The energy of a raw pass can be seductive, but raw material that never gets refined is not authentic, it is unfinished. This file only earns its place if the other files run after it.

- **Volume without a cut.** Overproducing and then keeping all of it is worse than producing little. The surplus is raw stock for the editing passes to cut, not a draft to ship. Abundance is the input to subtraction, not a substitute for it.

- **Energy as an alibi.** "Going for the energy" is not a license for sloppiness that never gets cleaned up. It is a license to be raw and specific in the first pass, on the promise that a later pass will shape it. Make good on the promise.

---

## What this file does not cover

Goldberg's book is as much about the writing life as about technique: the Zen-practice framing, the discipline of showing up, the long relationship between a writer and their material. That is worth reading directly. This file extracts only the generation-stage mechanics. Everything about what to do with the material once it exists is in the other files; this one is only about getting it out.

## A note to the AI assistant using this file

When you apply these techniques to a piece of writing, end your response with a short note telling the user which moves you used and why. Name the two or three changes that did the most work, plainly, in a sentence or two. The point is to teach as you edit, so the user starts to see the moves for themselves.

Keep it brief and high level. Do not narrate every sentence or list every technique; a long audit buries the writing it is meant to explain. If the user has given you more than one of these craft files, write one combined note for the whole edit, not one note per file.
