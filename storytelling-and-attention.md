# Storytelling and attention

*A model of why some writing holds attention and some loses it. The framework draws on research into curiosity and reward: George Loewenstein's information-gap theory of curiosity, reward-prediction-error work on surprise, Paul Zak's research on narrative and attention, Green and Brock on narrative transportation, Chip and Dan Heath's Made to Stick on why some ideas survive retelling, and Freytag's older account of dramatic shape. The four-beat packaging below is a framing popular in short-form storytelling; the ideas under it are older and well sourced.*

*This is the why-it-works layer beneath the other files. The craft files make a sentence land; this explains why a reader keeps reading at all. Pair it with the Truby file, which handles the macro-structure this operates inside.*

---

## The core idea

A story is not a sequence of facts. It is a sequence of prediction problems. The reader keeps going because their mind is working on one question: what is happening, why does it matter, and was I right?

That question gives you a four-beat loop. Each beat maps to a piece of how attention works: stakes assign value, the big question opens an information gap, the head fake delivers a prediction error, and the re-hook opens the next loop. Run the loop across a whole piece, and again inside each section, so the macro-arc is built from smaller arcs.

Each beat below has the same shape: its function, the rule that governs it, how it tends to fail, and an example.

---

## Beat 1: Stakes

**Function.** Make the reader understand what can be gained, lost, revealed, or wasted. Stakes can be practical, intellectual, reputational, aesthetic, or moral; they do not have to be life and death.

**The rule.** Do not surprise the reader before they know what they are trying to protect, solve, or understand. Some expected value has to be on the table before a surprise can matter.

**How it fails.** Vague stakes ("this is important") give the reader nothing to hold. The fix is a concrete consequence: name what breaks if the answer is no, and who pays for it.

**Example.** Vague: "This matters." Concrete: "If this rewrite fails, the page keeps losing readers in the first paragraph, and the best argument on the site never gets read."

---

## Beat 2: The big question

**Function.** Open one specific, unresolved question the reader can hold in working memory. Not the topic, the open question.

**The rule.** Curiosity needs partial knowledge. Give the reader enough of a model to feel the gap between what they know and what they want to know. Total ignorance is fog, not curiosity.

**How it fails.** A question stated as a topic ("this is about hiring") opens no loop. A question with no grounding ("what is postselection?") opens a gap the reader cannot feel. Give a recognizable pattern first, then expose the gap.

**Example.** Weak: "This is about hiring." Strong: "Can a small team ship faster by hiring less, not more?"

---

## Beat 3: The head fake

**Function.** Let the reader form a prediction, then show that the real structure is different. The head fake is a model correction, not a cheap twist.

**The rule.** A head fake should improve the reader's map, not just shock them. The obvious explanation is usually partly right; the move is to reveal the more useful explanation it was hiding.

**How it fails.** "Everything you know is wrong" is a shock with no payload; the reader's model does not improve, it just resets. Ask what the reader believed before the turn, what they believe after, and whether the new belief is more useful.

**Example.** "The problem was not the tool. It was that nobody had named who owned the decision." The surface explanation (a bad tool) gives way to the structural one (no owner).

---

## Beat 4: The re-hook

**Function.** Resolve enough to reward the attention the reader has spent, then open a sharper next question. A re-hook is a handoff from one curiosity gap to the next, not a cliffhanger.

**The rule.** Close the current loop before opening the next. Resolve, then re-open. Skip the resolution and the reader feels strung along instead of carried.

**How it fails.** "You won't believe what happened next" opens a loop without closing the last one, which reads as manipulation. The honest version answers the current question and lets the answer raise a harder one.

**Example.** "That fixed the speed problem. It left a harder one: who decides what 'done' means?"

---

## The expanded unit

For a longer piece, the loop expands into a sequence of beats you can lay out in order:

1. A concrete image or anchor to start.
2. Stakes: why it matters.
3. The big question: the loop opens.
4. A first model: let the reader predict.
5. The head fake: the model breaks.
6. A meaningful reveal: the prediction error pays off.
7. The re-hook: the next loop opens.
8. A compression line: the insight in a portable phrase.

This sits well alongside Made to Stick's account of why some ideas survive retelling: they tend to be simple, unexpected, concrete, and story-shaped. Freytag's older pyramid handles the macro-arc; the loop handles the micro-beats inside it.

## Sentence-level tactics

- **Curiosity gap:** "The strange part is not [the obvious thing]. It is [the hidden thing]."
- **Head fake:** "At first this looks like [category]. It is actually [deeper category]."
- **Stakes:** "If [claim] is true, then [consequence]."
- **Re-hook:** "That solves [problem A]. It leaves [problem B]."
- **Compression:** "[Thing] is not [surface role]. It is [deeper role]."

---

## Voice principles

- **Open hot, not loud.** A strong opening creates immediate work for the reader; it does not need hype. "The first mistake is assuming the team needs more freedom" beats "In today's fast-changing world."
- **Make every paragraph move the loop.** Each paragraph should raise a question, complicate one, answer one, or reveal that the question was incomplete. If it does none of these, cut it.
- **Alternate compression and expansion.** A concrete image, then the abstract meaning, then a concrete consequence, then a reframing. The rhythm keeps the reader from either drowning in detail or floating in abstraction.
- **Reward often.** A piece can sustain an open question only if it keeps paying out: a concrete example, a named pattern, a clean reversal, a phrase worth repeating.

---

## A structure pattern: the diagnostic essay

The most useful pattern for an argumentative piece runs: cold problem, stakes, big question, the common explanation, the head fake, the better model, the practical implications, and a re-hook to the broader system. In skeleton:

Everyone thinks the problem is X. It is not, or not first. The real question is what produced X. If the system rewards the wrong thing, then X is not a deviation, it is compliance. That changes the job: you are not cleaning up bad behavior, you are redesigning what made it sensible.

---

## Diagnostics

Run these on a draft:

- **Stakes:** can the reader say what is at risk? If not, add a consequence.
- **Question:** can they state the open question in one sentence? If not, sharpen it.
- **Head fake:** does the piece change the reader's model? If not, add a real turn.
- **Re-hook:** does each section build momentum into the next? If not, end sections on a sharper unresolved implication.
- **Transportation:** can the reader picture the situation? If not, add a concrete scene, person, or decision.
- **Stickiness:** can the reader repeat the core idea afterward? If not, compress it into a portable phrase.

---

## The ethical line

This material borrows from the language of addictive content, so it needs a boundary. Use it to clarify hard ideas, hold attention honestly, make stakes legible, and give readers emotional access to real problems. Do not use it to manufacture false urgency, hide weak evidence behind suspense, delay an obvious answer for effect, or open loops you never close. A good re-hook says "now that you understand this, the next question is unavoidable." A manipulative one withholds the answer because your attention is the product.

---

## The formula

Show the thing. Name the risk. Open the question. Let the reader predict. Break the prediction cleanly. Reward the update. Open the next, better question. Compress the insight into a sentence worth carrying.

Tighter: care, question, prediction, violation, meaning, next question.

## A note to the AI assistant using this file

When you apply these techniques to a piece of writing, end your response with a short note telling the user which moves you used and why. Name the two or three changes that did the most work, plainly, in a sentence or two. The point is to teach as you edit, so the user starts to see the moves for themselves.

Keep it brief and high level. Do not narrate every sentence or list every technique; a long audit buries the writing it is meant to explain. If the user has given you more than one of these craft files, write one combined note for the whole edit, not one note per file.
