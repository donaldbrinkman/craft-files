# Prose flow and cohesion

*Distilled from Joseph Williams, Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace. Where the Forsyth file covers sentence-level moves that intensify meaning, this covers paragraph-level moves that make meaning travel cleanly from one sentence to the next. Reach for it when a draft is correct sentence by sentence but reads as if it were assembled rather than written. Pair it with the Truby file for structure and the Forsyth file for the moments that have to land harder.*

---

## How to use this file

This is a process, not a checklist. Williams's framework works best applied as a sequence of passes over a draft, each asking one question and making one kind of change.

The passes, in order:

1. Character and action: are the grammatical subjects the real actors?
2. Old to new: does each sentence open with the known and end with the new?
3. Cohesion and coherence: do the sentences connect, and does the paragraph hold one point?
4. End-stress: does the most important information land at the end?
5. Concision: is every word earning its place?

Each pass below has the same shape: the principle, the diagnostic, the fix, and a worked example.

---

## Pass 1: Character and action

**The principle.** A sentence reads as clear when its grammatical subject is the entity doing the action and its main verb is the action itself. When the subject is a nominalization (a noun made from a verb) or an abstraction, and the real action is buried in a phrase, the sentence becomes harder to parse without becoming more precise.

**The diagnostic.** Find the grammatical subject. Is it the thing the sentence is actually about? Find the main verb. Is it the action the sentence is describing? If either answer is no, the sentence is misaligned.

**The fix.** Promote the real actor to subject position. Promote the real action to verb position. Demote or delete the nominalization.

**Example.** Misaligned: "The committee's evaluation of the proposal resulted in its rejection."

The subject is "evaluation," a nominalization. The real actor is the committee; the real action is evaluated.

Aligned: "The committee evaluated the proposal and rejected it."

The aligned version is shorter, but that is a side effect. The real gain is that the reader no longer has to translate the sentence to work out who did what.

---

## Pass 2: Old to new

**The principle.** Sentences read as connected when each one opens with information the reader already has and ends with information that is new. This is the rhythm readers expect; breaking it produces a friction they can feel but cannot always name.

**The diagnostic.** Read each sentence and find the first noun phrase. Is it something the prior sentence already established? If not, the sentence is starting cold.

**The fix.** Restructure so the sentence opens on familiar ground and closes on new ground. Sometimes a simple reorder; sometimes a split, so a bridge sentence can carry the connection.

**Example.** Friction: "Customer complaints drove a redesign of the checkout. Cart abandonment was the metric the redesign was built to move."

The second sentence opens on "cart abandonment," which is new, before circling back to the redesign. The bridge is buried.

Flow: "Customer complaints drove a redesign of the checkout. That redesign was built to move one metric: cart abandonment."

Now the second sentence opens on "that redesign," which is old and bridges immediately, and closes on the new information. The reader's eye and the argument move the same direction.

---

## Pass 3: Cohesion and coherence

**The principle.** Cohesion is the sentence-to-sentence connective tissue: each sentence visibly relating to the one before it. Coherence is the whole-paragraph effect: every sentence serving the paragraph's single point. Cohesion is local; coherence is global. A paragraph can be cohesive and still incoherent if every sentence connects to the last but the paragraph has no single point.

**The diagnostic.** For cohesion: can you draw a line from each sentence to the one before it and name the connection? For coherence: can you state the paragraph's single point in one sentence, and does every sentence contribute to it?

**The fix.** For cohesion failures, add bridges, reorder, or split. For coherence failures, cut the sentences that do not serve the point, or split the paragraph into two with two points.

**Example.** Cohesive but incoherent: "The team works from a shared process. Suggestions route through one coordinator. That coordinator also runs the weekly review. The reviews produced several of the team's working rules. Those rules live in a shared document."

Each sentence connects to the one before, but the paragraph is doing three jobs at once and develops none of them.

Coherent, one point: "The process works because one person is positioned to enforce it. Everyone else works behind walls of context they cannot see across; the coordinator is the one with the whole view. Without a real gatekeeper the process would be decoration. With one, it is the team's coordination layer."

One point, every sentence serving it.

---

## Pass 4: End-stress

**The principle.** English emphasizes what comes at the end. The reader's attention rises through the sentence and lands hardest on the final phrase. Important information at the end reads as emphasized; the same information in the middle reads as buried.

**The diagnostic.** What is the most important content in this sentence, and where does it sit? If the answer is the beginning or the middle, the sentence is fighting the reader's attention.

**The fix.** Move the emphatic content to the end. Sometimes that means restructuring the sentence; sometimes only moving a clause from front to back.

**Example.** Buried: "The plan's biggest weakness becomes its biggest strength under this framing, which is the point the review made."

The important claim sits in the middle; the sentence ends on attribution.

End-stressed: "The review made one point: under this framing, the plan's biggest weakness becomes its biggest strength."

Now the sentence ends on the claim. The attribution is setup; the claim is the payload.

---

## Pass 5: Concision

**The principle.** Every word is earning its place or stealing it. Words that do not earn their place repeat what nearby words already say, qualify a claim into vagueness, or perform a register move the sentence does not need.

**The diagnostic.** Read each sentence and ask of each word: if I cut this, what would the sentence lose? If the answer is nothing meaningful, cut it.

**The fix.** Cut. Williams catalogues the usual offenders: redundant pairs ("first and foremost"), redundant modifiers ("future plans," "completely finish"), redundant categories ("large in size"), empty words ("really," "basically," "actually," "very"), pompous diction ("utilize" for "use," "facilitate" for "help"), and throat-clearing ("it is important to note that," "what is interesting is that").

**Example.** Padded: "It is important to note that the team has, over the course of the project, gradually developed and refined a fairly comprehensive system for handling coordination."

Concise: "The team built a coordination system as the project went on."

The padded version carries throat-clearing, a redundant time phrase, a redundant pair ("developed and refined"), an empty hedge ("fairly comprehensive"), and a nominalization where a verb would do.

## How to layer Forsyth on top of Williams

Williams produces clean prose; Forsyth makes clean prose land harder. The order matters. Apply Williams first and get the prose reading as written rather than assembled. Apply Forsyth second, at the load-bearing moments, where a figure might do more than straight prose. Applying Forsyth first produces sentences that are clever but assembled. Clean prose earns the reader's attention; the figure then spends what the prose earned.

---

## Things to flinch at

- **The clarity trap.** This framework can produce prose so transparent it disappears, which is right for technical writing and wrong for argument. Some friction is good. Some end-weighted sentences want to be middle-weighted, because the middle-weighting produces interruption or surprise. Use Williams as a default, not a law.

- **The character-action fix that flattens voice.** Promoting actors to subjects is structurally right, but doing it to every sentence produces a flat run of declaratives with no variety. Vary the subjects deliberately. Sometimes the abstraction is the right subject, because it is genuinely the sentence's subject.

- **The deliberate old-to-new violation.** Opening a paragraph on a cold sentence is sometimes the right move; it forces the reader to lean in. The flow rule is the default; breaking it is a tool. Ask whether you broke it for effect or because you did not notice.

- **Concision applied to prose that wants extension.** Some sentences are doing work worth the words they cost. Cutting them to a shorter form loses what they were doing. Concision removes what is not earning its place; it does not minimize word count.

## What this file does not cover

Williams's book goes further than this: emphasis through subordination, the rhetoric of long sentences, the ethics of style, and how punctuation interacts with rhythm. The five passes above are the core operational moves for a revision pass. For the structure the prose carries, planting and payoff and the shape of an argument, see the Truby file. Williams handles the prose; Truby handles the structure.

## A note to the AI assistant using this file

When you apply these techniques to a piece of writing, end your response with a short note telling the user which moves you used and why. Name the two or three changes that did the most work, plainly, in a sentence or two. The point is to teach as you edit, so the user starts to see the moves for themselves.

Keep it brief and high level. Do not narrate every sentence or list every technique; a long audit buries the writing it is meant to explain. If the user has given you more than one of these craft files, write one combined note for the whole edit, not one note per file.
