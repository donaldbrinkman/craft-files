# Figures of speech: the repetition family

*Distilled from Mark Forsyth, The Elements of Eloquence (Icon Books, 2013). A companion to the surprise-family file. These figures all work by repetition: a word, a phrase, or a structure recurring on purpose, to build emphasis, rhythm, and memorability. Reach for them when a passage needs weight or a line needs to lodge in memory. They pair naturally with a flow pass (see the Williams file) and a structural pass (see the Truby file).*

*The examples below are for calibration, not for lifting. The figures work when they emerge from your material, not when they are imposed on it.*

---

## How to use this file

Each figure has the same four parts: definition, when to reach for it, how it tends to fail, and an example.

The repetition family shares one logic: deliberate recurrence. The reader has to feel the pattern was chosen. Accidental repetition is an error you cut in revision; chosen repetition is a figure you keep. The line between them is intent, and the reader can tell.

Reach for these at moments that deserve weight: a key claim, a closing, a series that should land as a single unit. Avoid them in dense informational passages, where repetition slows the delivery of facts.

---

## Anaphora

**Definition.** The same word or phrase repeated at the start of successive clauses or sentences. The repeated opening builds momentum and signals that the items belong together.

**When to reach for it.** A series of points you want to land as a rising set. Anaphora turns three separate sentences into one accumulating argument; the repeated opening is the thread the reader holds while the weight builds.

**How it tends to fail.** Too many repetitions. The force peaks around three or four and then decays into a tic. It also fails when the repeated phrase is not worth the emphasis it claims, so the structure promises weight the content does not deliver.

**Example.** "We tested the assumption. We tested the data. We tested the people who swore the data was clean."

The repeated "we tested" makes the three checks feel like one mounting investigation, and the third clause lands hardest because the pattern set it up.

---

## Epistrophe

**Definition.** The mirror of anaphora: the same word or phrase repeated at the end of successive clauses. The repeated ending drives a series toward a single shared conclusion.

**When to reach for it.** When several different subjects all arrive at the same fate or verdict, and you want that shared ending to ring. Epistrophe is the figure of inevitability; everything lands in the same place.

**How it tends to fail.** When the ending word is not the actual point, the repetition feels like a trick. And like anaphora, it fatigues past three or four.

**Example.** "The data did not support it. The team did not support it. In the end, the budget did not support it."

Three different reasons, one repeated verdict. The shared ending makes the conclusion feel forced by the facts rather than chosen by the writer.

---

## Anadiplosis

**Definition.** The last word or phrase of one clause becomes the first of the next, chaining the clauses together. The handoff makes a sequence feel causal and inevitable.

**When to reach for it.** Showing a chain: this leads to that, that leads to the next. Anadiplosis performs causation in its structure, so the reader feels the links before they analyze them.

**How it tends to fail.** When the links are not real. If "this" does not actually lead to "that," the chain is a rhetorical sleight rather than an argument. Overused, it also turns sing-song fast.

**Example.** "A clear brief earns trust. Trust earns autonomy. Autonomy earns the next, bigger brief."

Each clause hands its last word to the next, so the escalation reads as a mechanism, not a wish.

---

## Tricolon

**Definition.** Three parallel elements in a row. The rule of three: the smallest number that establishes a pattern, and the most satisfying to complete.

**When to reach for it.** A memorable triple, a closing list, a claim that wants the rhythm of three. The third element is the payoff slot; it can complete the pattern or break it for surprise.

**How it tends to fail.** A fourth element kills it; three is the number and four is a list. It also fails when the three things are not genuinely parallel, or when you padded to reach three and the weakest member shows.

**Example.** "It shipped late, it ran over budget, and it was exactly what the customer wanted."

Three parallel clauses, with the third turning against the first two. The tricolon sets the expectation; the swerve in the third spends it.

---

## Isocolon

**Definition.** Successive clauses of equal length and parallel structure. Where tricolon is about the count of three, isocolon is about symmetry: two or more clauses built to the same measure.

**When to reach for it.** Comparison and contrast. The matched structure makes two ideas feel weighed against each other, and any difference between them stands out because everything else is held constant.

**How it tends to fail.** False symmetry, where the clauses look parallel but are not, reads as a near-miss the reader can feel. And mechanical isocolon, symmetry for its own sake, sounds like an exercise.

**Example.** "The junior engineer asks how to build it. The senior engineer asks whether to build it."

Two clauses of the same shape; the single changed word, how against whether, carries the whole point.

---

## Diacope

**Definition.** A word repeated with a word or two in between. The brief interruption, then the return, gives the repeated word emphasis and a small charge of feeling.

**When to reach for it.** Single-point intensity. Diacope puts a frame around one word by saying it, pausing, and saying it again, which reads as conviction.

**How it tends to fail.** Overdone, it sounds theatrical. And if the interrupting words are flat, the return has nothing to land against.

**Example.** "Test, and then test again."

The repeated "test," split by "and then," lands harder than "test twice" would, because the structure enacts the doing-it-again.

---

## Epizeuxis

**Definition.** The same word repeated immediately, with nothing in between. The barest, most forceful repetition; pure emphasis.

**When to reach for it.** A single word that carries the weight of a whole point, when you want it hammered. Epizeuxis is the figure of insistence.

**How it tends to fail.** It is the easiest figure to overuse and the quickest to read as shouting. Once per piece is usually the ceiling, and three repetitions is usually the limit within one instance.

**Example.** "The priority is retention. Retention, retention, retention."

The immediate triple makes the word the only thing in the room. Used once, it is emphatic; used often, it is a tic.

---

## Polyptoton

**Definition.** Repeating a word in a different grammatical form: the same root reappearing as a different part of speech or inflection. The shared root links the ideas while the changed form moves the thought forward.

**When to reach for it.** When you want the effect of repetition without the flatness of saying the same word twice. Polyptoton rewards a reader who notices the root recurring in a new shape.

**How it tends to fail.** It is the most easily overclever of the family; pushed, it reads as wordplay for its own sake. And if the two forms do not actually illuminate each other, the cleverness is empty.

**Example.** "A leader who will not lead is just a title."

"Leader" and "lead" share a root and pull against each other, so the sentence makes its point in the friction between the noun and the verb.

---

## How to layer these in

In a revision pass, after the argument is clear and the prose is clean, work the repetition figures in roughly this order:

1. **Tricolon first.** Most pieces have a natural triple somewhere. Find it and let it be three.
2. **Anaphora and epistrophe for series.** A run of points that wants emphasis takes a repeated opening; a run that arrives at one verdict takes a repeated ending.
3. **Anadiplosis for chains.** Where the argument is genuinely causal, let the clauses hand off their last words.
4. **Diacope and epizeuxis for single-point intensity.** One word that has to carry weight.
5. **Isocolon for balanced contrast, and polyptoton sparingly,** for the moments that can carry a little wit.

The cardinal rule: repetition works by being deliberate. The reader has to feel the pattern was chosen, not stumbled into.

---

## Things to flinch at

- **Repetition fatigue.** Anaphora past four units, epizeuxis past three, and the force inverts into a tic. These figures have short ceilings.

- **The empty pattern.** Repeating a phrase that was not worth repeating borrows emphasis the content has not earned. The structure should follow the weight, not manufacture it.

- **The sing-song.** Stack three repetition figures in one paragraph and prose turns to chant. One or two per passage; let plain sentences carry the space between.

- **Accidental repetition wearing the costume.** If you repeated a word by accident, that is an edit, not a figure. Cut it. A figure the reader senses you did not intend reads as a mistake, not a move.

---

## When to leave them alone

- **Dense informational passages.** Repetition spends time and slows the delivery of facts. Where the job is to convey information cleanly, drop the figures.

- **Where concision matters more than weight.** Repetition buys emphasis with words. Sometimes the budget does not allow it.

- **Claims that already land.** A strong claim does not need the scaffolding. Repetition on top of it reads as overselling something the reader had already accepted.

---

## What this file does not cover

Forsyth covers more repetition-adjacent figures than these eight: epanalepsis (the same word opening and closing a clause), symploce (anaphora and epistrophe at once), and polysyndeton and asyndeton (deliberately repeating or dropping conjunctions). The surprise family, the figures that work by reversal, is in the companion Forsyth file. For the rest of the book's families, sound, substitution, and arrangement, and for any figure outside both files, Silva Rhetoricae (rhetoric.byu.edu) is a free and comprehensive reference, organized by both name and function.

## A note to the AI assistant using this file

When you apply these techniques to a piece of writing, end your response with a short note telling the user which moves you used and why. Name the two or three changes that did the most work, plainly, in a sentence or two. The point is to teach as you edit, so the user starts to see the moves for themselves.

Keep it brief and high level. Do not narrate every sentence or list every technique; a long audit buries the writing it is meant to explain. If the user has given you more than one of these craft files, write one combined note for the whole edit, not one note per file.
