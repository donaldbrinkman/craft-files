Handing my writing craft to an AI was supposed to make me lazier. It made me a better writer.

For twenty years I read the craft books. Forsyth on figures of speech, Williams on flow, Truby on structure, Orwell on cutting. More recently, the research on how stories hold attention, the kind that lives in talks and videos now. The problem with all of it is that the knowledge sits in your head and surfaces, when it surfaces at all, by luck.

So I distilled them. Five short files, each one turned from "here is the idea" into "here is when to reach for it, how it fails, and what to watch out for". Then I dropped them into the thing that refines my first drafts. I start with an undisciplined stream-of-consciousness dump of my idea and ask the agent to apply the rules.

Two things happened. The revisions stopped sounding generic, because the model had real moves instead of vibes. And I got sharper, because I told each file to make the assistant show its work. It doesn't just refine now. It tells me which moves it used and why, every time. Twenty years of half-remembered craft, narrated back to me while I work.

That is the whole trick. The craft was never the scarce part. Turning it into something a machine can act on, and a person can learn from, was.

I wrote this post with those five files. Including this one: the first line is a reversal, the shape is borrowed from Truby, the last pass cut every word that wasn't earning its place. The file told me so.

They're linked below. Take them. Drop them into your assistant.

Worst case, your AI writes better. Best case, you do.
